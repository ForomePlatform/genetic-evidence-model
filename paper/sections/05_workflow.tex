\section{Human--AI Annotation Workflow and Evaluation}
\label{sec:workflow}

The model is a schema, but a schema is only as useful as the
annotations it supports. We designed and piloted an annotation
workflow whose central commitment is that every annotation be
\emph{auditable}: each assertion is anchored to the specific text that
justifies it, each dimension value is defensible against the schema's
activation conditions, and every judgement call that a downstream
consumer might contest carries an explicit reviewer flag. The same
commitment extends to AI assistance: when an AI system drafts an
annotation, its uncertainties and assumptions are not silently
absorbed but recorded alongside the annotation for curator review.

This section describes the protocol (\cref{sec:workflow-protocol}),
reports coverage and flag statistics from the six-paper pilot
(\cref{sec:workflow-coverage,sec:workflow-flags}), and examines the
extension-promotion rule that determines when a candidate schema
extension becomes first-class (\cref{sec:workflow-promotion}). We
treat the pilot as a \emph{feasibility study} rather than a benchmark:
six papers with a single annotator are not enough to support claims
about inter-annotator agreement or generalisability. The pilot does
show that the workflow's epistemic commitments are practical to meet
and that the schema's strain points are surfaced rather than hidden.

\subsection{Protocol}
\label{sec:workflow-protocol}

Annotations are authored in two tracks.

\paragraph{Manual track.}
Four of the six publications (Jossin, Davis, Nelson, Gupta) were
annotated manually. A curator read each paper and marked relevant PDF
passages as highlights and callouts. A provided script extracted these
into a structured JSON record. The curator's summary table of
dimension values and passage-level callouts was then converted into a
YAML annotation conforming to the schema. Whenever this conversion
required an extra judgement (for example, mapping the free-text
``localisation and protein expression'' to the enumeration values
\dimval{Localization} and \dimval{Existence}), the change was recorded
inline as a \texttt{normalization\_note} with \texttt{type =
ai\_normalization} for later audit.

\paragraph{AI-drafted track.}
For two publications (Duerr, Inouye) the annotation was drafted
directly by an AI assistant\footnote{Claude Opus 4.7 Adaptive.}
working from the PDF text. The AI was given the schema
(\texttt{dimensions.md}), the ground-truth annotations of the four
manual papers, and the PDFs themselves, and produced YAML annotations
in the same format. When the AI judged that a mapping was uncertain or
that a judgement call ought to be verified by a human reviewer, it
recorded the uncertainty inline (see \cref{sec:workflow-flags}). The
curator then reviewed each AI-drafted annotation
assertion-by-assertion, flagging any that misrepresented the paper or
misused the schema.

\paragraph{Source anchoring.}
In both tracks, every \GeneticEvidenceAssertion{} carries a
\texttt{source\_span} pointing to a specific page and a short key
phrase (typically under 15 words) from the underlying text. The phrase
is chosen to be long enough for a reader to locate the passage by
search and short enough to stay within fair-use norms for copyrighted
publisher material. Source anchoring is not optional: an annotation
without source spans is rejected at validation time. Across the
six-paper pilot, 65 of 65 assertions (100\%) are source-anchored; this
is by construction, not by observation.

The always-required dimensions are fully populated by construction,
since the schema rejects annotations that leave them empty. The
conditional dimensions are more informative, and three patterns are
worth noting.

First, \emph{high-population} dimensions such as Measurement Target
(10/11) and Organism (7/8) suggest that their activation conditions
are well calibrated: when the rule says the dimension should apply, it
almost always does. Second, \emph{zero-population} dimensions such as
Mode of Inheritance (0/1) and Mendelian Segregation (0/1) indicate
boundary cases. The single applicable item for each is Duerr's
family-based transmission disequilibrium analysis, a complex-trait
study where the annotator was unsure whether \dimval{Complex} ought to
be a first-class value in the Mode-of-Inheritance enumeration
(\cref{sec:workflow-flags}, \textit{ai\_uncertainty} on Duerr GE-3).
Third, \emph{low-but-nonzero} dimensions such as Gene Relation (1/11)
reveal schema shortcomings: in the eleven \dimval{Gene Function}
evidence items, only one had a gene relation that fit the current
three-value enumeration (\dimval{X regulates Y}, \dimval{X inhibits
Y}, \dimval{X has same function as Y}); the remaining ten mostly
involved physical binding or trafficking, which the enumeration does
not cover. This motivates the candidate extension CE-J3 surfaced in
the Jossin annotation (\cref{sec:discussion}).

These coverage statistics are consistent with the activation rules: no
dimension is populated where the rule says it should not be, and the
few cases where a required dimension is empty are each accompanied by
an explicit flag. Coverage alone does not tell us whether the
annotations are \emph{correct} (that would require a second
annotator); it only shows that the schema's structural requirements
are being met.

\subsection{Flag Taxonomy}
\label{sec:workflow-flags}

The two tracks use different flag vocabularies because they
represent different epistemic situations.

\paragraph{Manual track: three flag types.}
When a curator-authored annotation diverged from the curator's own
summary table, or when an AI review of the manual annotation detected
a mapping issue, we recorded one of three flag types. A
\texttt{reviewer\_disagreement} marks a clear factual correction: in
the Davis 2011 annotation, the curator's summary listed the organism
set as \{mouse, zebrafish\}, but the paper also reported a rat retinal
electroporation experiment, and the AI reviewer proposed adding
\dimval{Rat}. A \texttt{reviewer\_suggestion} marks an optional
addition whose inclusion depends on curator preference; HPO terms for
patient phenotypes are a recurring case, flagged in Davis, Nelson, and
Gupta. A \texttt{reviewer\_query} marks a case where the mapping is
itself unclear and curator input is needed: the Nelson summary table
gave \texttt{specificity\_of\_phenotype = SNV}, which is semantically
odd (SNV is a variant type, not a phenotype-specificity descriptor),
and the annotation preserves the original value with a query rather
than silently correcting it.

\paragraph{AI-drafted track: two flag types.}
When no prior ground truth exists, an AI-drafted annotation has
nothing to \emph{disagree} with, so the AI track uses a narrower
vocabulary. An \texttt{ai\_uncertainty} marks a case where the AI was
unsure about a schema mapping and prefers human input (for example,
Duerr's \dimval{Complex} value for Mode of Inheritance). An
\texttt{ai\_assumption} marks a judgement call made without strong
evidence that a reviewer should verify. Duerr GE-6 is a typical case:
the AI collected cited mouse-model work into a \GeneticEvidence{}
item but flagged that it is unclear whether evidence \emph{cited} by a
paper belongs in that paper's annotation or whether citation chains
need a separate representational layer. Such decisions are recorded
rather than resolved silently.

The totals themselves are less informative than the distribution.
Disagreements are rare (one across the four manual papers), which

six of each, typically for edge cases (HPO-term candidates,
knockout-type vs.\ mode-of-inheritance scoping, ``SNV in the
phenotype slot'') where human judgement beyond the schema is
needed. For the AI-drafted papers, Duerr generated three
uncertainties (knowledge-domain scoping for GE-1, mode-of-inheritance
enumeration for GE-3, the cited-vs-generated boundary for GE-6) and
two assumptions (source-phrase selection and the placeholder choice
for GE-3); Inouye generated no uncertainties at all (the AI
identified each stress-test point as a candidate-extension
rather than an uncertain mapping) and two assumptions about
measurement-target encoding.

\subsection{The Extension-Promotion Rule}
\label{sec:workflow-promotion}

The workflow adopts a deliberately conservative rule for when a
candidate extension enters the schema. A gap seen in a single paper is
recorded in that paper's \texttt{candidate\_extensions} block only. A
gap seen \emph{independently} in a second paper is promoted to a
first-class dimension or enumeration value. The aim is to let
idiosyncrasies remain local while growing the schema only in response
to convergent evidence.

In the six-paper pilot this rule promoted two extensions:
\emph{Phenotype Scale}, first proposed in Jossin to distinguish
molecular, histological, and organismal phenotypes and then exercised
in Davis, Nelson, and later papers; and \emph{Variant Ascertainment},
first proposed in Davis to distinguish patient-observed from
database-selected variants and then exercised in Nelson, Duerr, and
Inouye. Both now appear in the core dimensions
(\cref{tab:dimensions-core}), the latter conditional on
\mbox{\dimval{Target Type}~=~\dimval{Variant}}. Thirteen other
candidates remain unpromoted, pending either a second encounter or an
explicit curator decision.

The rule also blocks false positives. In Duerr, AI initially proposed
a new dimension for negative-polarity assertions (for example, ``no
significant association at IL12RB1, IL23A, or IL12B''). On review the
curator observed that an \EvidenceAssertion{} is just a predicate and
already supports absence as well as presence, so no change to the
schema was needed and the candidate was retracted. Under a looser
single-paper rule this would have left the model with a redundant
polarity field of little practical use.

\subsection{Discussion of Methodology}
\label{sec:workflow-discussion}

Three features of the workflow bear brief emphasis. First, schema gaps
are treated as \emph{data}: AI and curator annotations record
candidate extensions, uncertainties, and disagreements inline rather
than silently coercing claims into existing enumerations. Second, the
manual and AI tracks reflect different epistemic roles, primary versus
secondary knowledge work, and use distinct flag vocabularies to record
provenance. Third, the six-paper pilot is intentionally small; it
tests feasibility and the adequacy of the flag vocabularies, not
inter-annotator agreement or large-scale scalability, which we leave
for future work.
