\section{Human--AI Annotation Workflow and Evaluation}
\label{sec:workflow}

We designed and piloted an annotation workflow whose central
commitment is that every annotation is \emph{auditable}: each
assertion is anchored to the specific text that justifies it, each
dimension value is defensible against the schema's activation
conditions, and every judgement call that a downstream consumer
might contest carries an explicit reviewer or AI flag. The same
commitment extends to AI assistance: when an AI system drafts an
annotation, its uncertainties and assumptions are not silently
absorbed but recorded alongside the annotation for curator review.
The manual and AI tracks play different epistemic roles (primary
versus secondary knowledge work) and use distinct flag vocabularies
to record provenance.

We treat the pilot as a \emph{feasibility study} rather than a
benchmark. Six papers with a single annotator are not enough to
support claims about inter-annotator agreement or generalisability.
The pilot does show that the workflow's epistemic commitments are
practical to meet and that the schema's strain points are surfaced
rather than hidden.

\subsection{Protocol and Coverage}
\label{sec:workflow-protocol}

Annotations are authored in two tracks (\cref{fig:workflow}).

\paragraph{Manual track.}
Four of the six publications (Jossin, Davis, Nelson, Gupta) were
annotated manually by Vladimir Seplyarskiy, a researcher in human and
population genetics. The curator read each paper, marked relevant
passages in the PDF as highlights and callouts, exported these via a
helper script into a structured JSON record, and mapped the result,
together with a per-paper summary row, into a YAML annotation conforming
to the schema. When this conversion required a normalisation step (for
example, mapping the free-text ``localisation and protein expression''
to the enumeration values \dimval{Localization} and
\dimval{Existence}), the change was recorded inline as a
\texttt{normalization\_note} with \texttt{type = ai\_normalization} so
that later audits can see both the original and the normalised value.
The manual-track protocol is described in more detail in
Supplementary Note~SN2.

\paragraph{AI-drafted track.}
For two publications (Duerr, Inouye) the annotation was drafted directly
from the PDFs by an AI assistant operating under an executable
annotation skill \cite{anthropic_skills}. The assistant was given the
schema (\texttt{dimensions.md}), the ground-truth annotations of the
four manual papers as exemplars, and the PDFs themselves, and produced
YAML annotations in the same format. When the assistant judged that a
mapping was uncertain or that a judgement call ought to be verified by a
human reviewer, it recorded this inline as either
\texttt{ai\_uncertainty} or \texttt{ai\_assumption}. These AI-drafted
annotations were then reviewed assertion by assertion by one of the
authors, whose expertise is in genomic data curation and the evidence
model rather than in genetics domain science. The review checked
faithful representation of the source and conformance to the schema, and
did not re-adjudicate the underlying science, for which the manual
annotations provide the domain-expert ground truth. The executable
skills and review protocol are described in Supplementary Notes~SN2 and
SN3.

\paragraph{Source anchoring.}
In both tracks, every assertion (the \texttt{assertion} property of a
\GeneticEvidence{} item) carries a \texttt{source\_span} pointing to a
specific page and a short key phrase (typically under fifteen words)
from the underlying text. The phrase is chosen to be long enough for a
reader to locate the passage by search and short enough to stay within
fair-use norms for copyrighted publisher material. Source anchoring is
not optional: an annotation with any assertion lacking a source span is
rejected at validation time. Across the six-paper pilot, 95 of 95
assertions (100\%) are source-anchored; this is by construction, not by
observation.

\subsection{Flags and Extension Promotion}
\label{sec:workflow-flags}
\label{sec:workflow-promotion}

The two tracks use small flag vocabularies to make epistemic provenance
explicit.

\paragraph{Manual track.}
We use three reviewer flags. A \texttt{reviewer\_disagreement} marks a
clear factual correction, such as an organism-set correction in
Davis~2011. A \texttt{reviewer\_suggestion} marks an optional addition
whose inclusion depends on curator preference, such as HPO terms for
patient phenotypes in Davis, Nelson, and Gupta. A
\texttt{reviewer\_query} marks a case where the mapping to the schema
is itself unclear and should not be silently corrected; for example, the
Nelson summary gave \texttt{specificity\_of\_phenotype = SNV}, which
does not fit the dimension's semantics.

\paragraph{AI-drafted track.}
With no prior ground truth to disagree with, the AI track uses two
flags. An \texttt{ai\_uncertainty} marks a mapping the assistant judged
unreliable and prefers a human decision on, such as a borderline
assignment of Mode of Inheritance. An \texttt{ai\_assumption} records a
judgement call made without strong textual support, such as whether
cited mouse-model evidence should be represented as a separate
\GeneticEvidence{} item or treated as background context. Across the
six-paper pilot, manual-track annotations carried five queries, six
suggestions, and one disagreement, while AI-track annotations carried
six uncertainties and six assumptions. For the nine AI-drafted items
across Duerr and Inouye, none was rejected: one was approved as drafted,
three were approved with non-blocking flags, and five were edited. The
later-protocol Inouye draft required fewer edits than the earlier Duerr
draft, but the papers differ in genre so this is suggestive rather than
a controlled comparison. Edits mainly affected dimension assignment and
credibility calibration rather than the factual content of assertions,
and a small number of higher-level items such as a paper's overall
thesis or a cited translational study were added by the reviewer.
Per-paper counts and detailed rationales are given in Supplementary
Note~SN4.

\paragraph{Extension promotion.}
The workflow adopts a conservative rule for evolving the schema. A
representational gap seen in a single paper is recorded only in that
paper's \texttt{candidate\_extensions} block. A gap seen independently
in a second paper is promoted to a first-class dimension or
enumeration value. In the six-paper pilot this rule promoted two
extensions: \emph{Phenotype Scale}, first proposed in Jossin and then
reused in Davis, Nelson, and later papers; and \emph{Variant
Ascertainment}, first proposed in Davis and then reused in Nelson and
Duerr. Both now appear in the vocabulary
(\cref{tab:dimensions-core,tab:dimensions-cond}): Phenotype Scale among
the always-required dimensions, Variant Ascertainment among the
conditional ones (required when the target type is \dimval{Variant}).
Fifteen other candidates remain unpromoted, pending a
second independent use or an explicit curator decision. The rule also
prevents premature additions. For example, a proposed polarity
dimension for negative assertions in Duerr was withdrawn once review
confirmed that the existing assertion predicate already represents
absence as well as presence.
