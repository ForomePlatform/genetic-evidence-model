\section{Human--AI Annotation Workflow and Evaluation}
\label{sec:workflow}

We designed and piloted an annotation workflow whose central
commitment is that every annotation be \emph{auditable}: each
assertion is anchored to the specific text that justifies it, each
dimension value is defensible against the schema's activation
conditions, and every judgement call that a downstream consumer
might contest carries an explicit reviewer flag. The same
commitment extends to AI assistance: when an AI system drafts an
annotation, its uncertainties and assumptions are not silently
absorbed but recorded alongside the annotation for curator review.
The manual and AI tracks below reflect different epistemic roles,
primary versus secondary knowledge work, and use distinct flag
vocabularies to record provenance.

We treat the pilot as a \emph{feasibility study} rather than a
benchmark: six papers with a single annotator are not enough to
support claims about inter-annotator agreement or generalisability.
The pilot does show that the workflow's epistemic commitments are
practical to meet and that the schema's strain points are surfaced
rather than hidden.

\subsection{Protocol and Coverage}
\label{sec:workflow-protocol}

Annotations are authored in two tracks (\cref{fig:workflow}).

\paragraph{Manual track.}
Four of the six publications (Jossin, Davis, Nelson, Gupta) were
annotated manually by Vladimir Seplyarskiy, a researcher in human and
population genetics specialising in human germline mutation and
mutational processes (Assistant Professor at UT~Southwestern; formerly
Harvard Medical School and Brigham and Women's Hospital). This
expertise covers the human-genetics and population-genetics knowledge
domains the corpus exercises. The curator read each paper and marked relevant
PDF passages as highlights and callouts. A provided script extracted
these into a structured JSON record. The curator's summary table of
dimension values and passage-level callouts was then converted into
a YAML annotation conforming to the schema. Whenever this conversion
required an extra judgement (for example, mapping the free-text
``localisation and protein expression'' to the enumeration values
\dimval{Localization} and \dimval{Existence}), the change was
recorded inline as a \texttt{normalization\_note} with
\texttt{type = ai\_normalization} for later audit.

\paragraph{AI-drafted track.}
For two publications (Duerr, Inouye) the annotation was drafted
directly by an AI assistant\footnote{Claude Opus 4.7 Adaptive.}
working from the PDF text. Both the drafting and the subsequent
review were carried out under executable \emph{skills}~\cite{anthropic_skills},
versioned bundles that package the annotation and review protocols for
an AI assistant to apply (Supplementary Notes~SN2 and~SN3). The AI was given the schema
(\texttt{dimensions.md}), the ground-truth annotations of the four
manual papers, and the PDFs themselves, and produced YAML
annotations in the same format. When the AI judged that a mapping
was uncertain or that a judgement call ought to be verified by a
human reviewer, it recorded the uncertainty inline. These AI-drafted annotations were then reviewed
assertion-by-assertion by one of the authors, whose expertise is in
genomic data curation and the evidence model rather than in genetics
domain science, working with Claude Opus 4.8; the review checked
faithful representation of the source and conformance to the schema,
flagging any assertion that misrepresented the paper or misused the
model, and did not re-adjudicate the underlying science, for which
the manual annotations provide the domain-expert ground truth.

\paragraph{Source anchoring.}
In both tracks, every assertion (the \texttt{assertion} property of a
\GeneticEvidence{} item) carries a \texttt{source\_span} pointing to a
specific page and a short key
phrase (typically under 15 words) from the underlying text. The
phrase is chosen to be long enough for a reader to locate the
passage by search and short enough to stay within fair-use norms
for copyrighted publisher material. Source anchoring is not
optional: an annotation without source spans is rejected at
validation time. Across the six-paper pilot, 69 of 69 assertions
(100\%) are source-anchored; this is by construction, not by
observation.

\subsection{Flags and Extension Promotion}
\label{sec:workflow-flags}
\label{sec:workflow-promotion}

The two tracks use different flag vocabularies because they represent
different epistemic situations.

\paragraph{Manual track: three flag types.}
A \texttt{reviewer\_disagreement} marks a clear factual correction
(for example, an organism-set correction in Davis 2011). A
\texttt{reviewer\_suggestion} marks an optional addition whose
inclusion depends on curator preference (HPO terms for patient
phenotypes are recurrent cases in Davis, Nelson, and Gupta). A
\texttt{reviewer\_query} marks a case where the mapping is itself
unclear: the Nelson summary gave
\texttt{specificity\_of\_phenotype = SNV}, which is semantically
odd, and the annotation preserves the original with a query rather
than silently correcting it.

\paragraph{AI-drafted track: two flag types.}
With no prior ground truth to disagree with, the AI track uses a
narrower vocabulary. An \texttt{ai\_uncertainty} marks a case where
the AI was unsure about a schema mapping and prefers human input
(for example, Duerr's \dimval{Complex} value for Mode of
Inheritance). An \texttt{ai\_assumption} marks a judgement call made
without strong evidence that a reviewer should verify, such as
whether evidence \emph{cited} by a paper belongs in that paper's
annotation or warrants a separate representational layer (Duerr
GE-6). Such decisions are recorded rather than resolved silently.

Across the six-paper pilot, manual-track flags numbered 5
queries, 6 suggestions, and 1 disagreement; AI-track flags
numbered 3 uncertainties and 4 assumptions.
The distribution is more informative than the totals. Disagreements
are rare (one across the four manual papers), which is expected when
the annotator also authored the summary being compared against.
Suggestions and queries are the workhorses, six of each, typically
for edge cases where human judgement beyond the schema is needed.
For the AI-drafted papers, Inouye generated no uncertainties at all
(the AI identified every stress-test point as a candidate extension
rather than an uncertain mapping), while Duerr generated three
uncertainties concentrated on scoping and enumeration decisions.
Per-paper flag counts are in the coverage file in the
repository.

\paragraph{AI-drafted track: review outcomes.}
Both AI-drafted annotations were reviewed assertion by assertion by one
of the authors against the review protocol, with every correction
recorded in the public review logs and summarised in Supplementary
Note~SN4. Of the nine AI-drafted items across the two papers, none was
rejected, one was approved as drafted, three carried only non-blocking
flags, and five were marked for edit. Two patterns stand out. The draft
written under the current protocol needed far less correction than the
earlier one, with two of its three items accepted without edits against
four of six edited, and all thirty-seven of its cited spans verbatim;
the two papers differ, so this is suggestive rather than a controlled
comparison. And in both papers the evidence the reviewer had to add was
not a granular finding but a higher-level claim, the paper's overall
thesis in one case and its cited translational evidence in the other,
which suggests the draft is most reliable on specific, directly stated
claims. The remaining edits fell in dimension assignment and credibility
calibration, not in the evidence itself, and no item was found to
misrepresent its source.

\paragraph{The extension-promotion rule.}
The workflow adopts a deliberately conservative rule for when a
candidate extension enters the schema. A gap seen in a single paper
is recorded in that paper's \texttt{candidate\_extensions} block
only. A gap seen \emph{independently} in a second paper is promoted
to a first-class dimension or enumeration value. In the six-paper
pilot this rule promoted two extensions: \emph{Phenotype Scale},
first proposed in Jossin and then exercised in Davis, Nelson, and
later papers; and \emph{Variant Ascertainment}, first proposed in
Davis and then exercised in Nelson and Duerr. Both now appear in the core
dimensions (\cref{tab:dimensions-core}), the latter conditional on
\mbox{\dimval{Target Type}~=~\dimval{Variant}}. Fifteen other
candidates remain unpromoted, pending either a second encounter or
an explicit curator decision. The rule also blocks false positives.
In Duerr, AI initially proposed a new dimension for negative-polarity
assertions (for example, ``no significant association at IL12RB1,
IL23A, or IL12B''). On review the curator observed that an
assertion is just a predicate and already supports absence
as well as presence, so no change to the schema was needed and the
candidate was retracted. Under a looser single-paper rule this would
have left the model with a redundant polarity field of little
practical use.
