\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

Two features of the proposal bear brief recapitulation. The
always-required vocabulary is small, and its conditional-activation
mechanism keeps the extended vocabulary compact without sacrificing
expressive power. And every assertion in every annotation is
anchored to a specific source span in its underlying publication,
making each annotation independently auditable.

\subsection{Alignment with the ICBO 2026 Theme}
\label{sec:disc-icbo}

The conference call asks for contributions that advance trustworthy,
interpretable, and AI-ready semantic infrastructure. Every schema
conformance requirement is formalised as a SHACL shape, every
activation condition is machine-checkable, and source anchoring
makes each assertion verifiable against its origin rather than
against a style guide. The flag vocabularies of
\cref{sec:workflow-flags} make provenance explicit: a reader can
tell whether a value was drawn from the author's summary, proposed
by a reviewer, added as an AI assumption, or left deliberately
unpopulated. A broader framing is that the schema is designed to
make literature review tractable as a \emph{semantic-parsing}
problem, the mapping of natural-language claims to a shared,
source-anchored representation. Modern large language models have
become substantially more capable at semantic parsing than they
were only a few years ago; our flag vocabularies were designed with
a parser's three possible outcomes in mind, a successful mapping,
a request for human disambiguation, or a proposal to extend the
target language. Parsing the biomedical literature, however, is not
something a language model can do in isolation: the target of each
parse is an ontology, and the quality of the parse depends on the
quality of the ontological grounding. We see the present
contribution as one increment along a longer path, building on
decades of biomedical-ontology work (ECO, SEPIO, HPO, IAO, OBI,
VRS, and others), not as a replacement for any of it.

\subsection{Model Extensions Surfaced by Annotation}
\label{sec:disc-extensions}

The pilot surfaced two candidate dimensions that were exercised by
multiple papers and promoted to the always-required vocabulary
(Phenotype Scale and Variant Ascertainment), plus thirteen further
candidate extensions that have been recorded but not yet promoted
under the two-papers rule (\cref{sec:workflow-promotion}). The
pattern of candidates is itself informative and is summarised in
\cref{tab:candidates}; per-candidate rationale, affected dimensions,
and proposed enumeration values are documented in a companion
\texttt{schema/EXTENSIONS.md} in the repository.\footnote{\url{https://github.com/ForomePlatform/genetic-evidence-model/blob/master/schema/EXTENSIONS.md}}
The Gupta annotation, which surfaced no candidate extensions, is
worth naming as a positive finding: the paper reports a family with
an X-linked splice-site mutation and careful pedigree segregation,
the canonical evidence shape the model was designed for, and the
schema accommodates it without strain.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:disc-limitations}

The pilot has significant limitations. Six papers and a single
annotator do not support claims about inter-annotator agreement or
generalisability; we treat the work as a feasibility study, not a
benchmark. The model has not been released as a fully
OWL-formalised ontology: the present SHACL distribution covers
class declarations, dimension enumerations, and activation rules,
but not the axiomatisation that classification and subsumption
inference would require. The evaluation is not a clinical
deployment: although the model is motivated by variant
interpretation in clinical-genomics programs, the pilot stops short
of applying the model to a live workflow. And the corpus is skewed
toward research papers about disease genes and variants; the
candidate extensions of \cref{tab:candidates} should be read as
findings from \emph{this} corpus, not a complete survey of the
schema's coverage gaps.

\subsection{Future Work}
\label{sec:disc-future}

Three near-term directions: annotate a second corpus selected to
exercise the strongest candidate extensions; generate OWL, SHACL,
JSON Schema, and LinkML renderings from a single source-of-truth to
open the schema to the OBO and Bridge2AI ecosystems; and carry out
a clinical-deployment study to turn the feasibility claim into a
utility claim.

\section{Availability of Code, Data, and Artefacts}
\label{sec:availability}

All artefacts referenced in this paper are open-source and
available in the project repository at
\url{https://github.com/ForomePlatform/genetic-evidence-model}.
This includes the SHACL schema, the human-readable dimension
reference (\texttt{dimensions.md}), per-paper annotations for all
six pilot publications, case reports for the two featured
annotations (Duerr 2006 and Inouye 2018), the companion
\texttt{schema/EXTENSIONS.md}, the SHACL walkthrough
(\texttt{schema/examples.md}), and the extraction tooling used to
convert PDF highlights and callouts into annotation scaffolds. The
namespace \url{https://w3id.org/genetic-evidence-model/} resolves
to the same repository.
