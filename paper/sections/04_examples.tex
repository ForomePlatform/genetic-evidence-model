\section{Illustrative Examples from the Literature}
\label{sec:examples}

To demonstrate the model's coverage and to see where it strains, we
applied it to six published genetic-evidence papers that span the
major epistemic shapes encountered in literature-based variant
interpretation (\cref{tab:corpus}). Four of the six were annotated
manually from curator PDF highlights and callouts and are treated as
ground truth. The remaining two were drafted by an AI assistant
working from the PDF text alone, with curator review serving as the
evaluation signal. All six annotations are available in the project
repository; here we discuss two in detail.

We describe two of the papers in detail here and summarise the others
more briefly. Duerr et al.\ 2006 (\cref{sec:ex-duerr}) is a textbook
GWAS with an evidence shape that our proposed approach accommodates
cleanly; it serves as our reference example of a classical
variant-level association study. Inouye et al.\ 2018
(\cref{sec:ex-inouye}) is a polygenic risk score study with an
evidence shape that deliberately strains the model; its
annotation surfaces a cluster of candidate extensions that we return
to in \cref{sec:discussion}. The other four annotations (Jossin,
Davis, Nelson, Gupta) are documented in the repository's
\texttt{annotations/} directory, and we refer to them below only as
needed to illustrate specific methodological points. Full annotations
for the two featured papers, with per-assertion source spans and
annotator commentary, are distributed as standalone case reports in
the repository's \texttt{case-reports/} directory.\footnote{\url{https://github.com/ForomePlatform/genetic-evidence-model/tree/master/case-reports}}

\subsection{A Genome-Wide Association Study: Duerr et al.\ 2006}
\label{sec:ex-duerr}

Duerr and colleagues identified the \textit{IL23R} association with
inflammatory bowel disease by typing 308{,}332 autosomal SNPs in 547
non-Jewish European-ancestry cases with ileal Crohn's disease against
548 matched controls. After observing a genome-wide-significant
association, they carried out independent replication in a
Jewish-ancestry cohort and family-based transmission disequilibrium
testing in 883 nuclear families. The paper's central finding, that the
Arg381Gln coding variant rs11209026 confers protection against
Crohn's disease with an odds ratio of 0.26 in the discovery cohort, is
the kind of result the model was designed to represent: a
variant-level claim grounded in population-genetic methodology, with
explicit cohort descriptors, a multiple-testing adjustment, and
external replication.

Our annotation decomposes the paper into six \GeneticEvidence{} items
covering, respectively, the GWAS discovery, the Jewish-cohort
replication, the family-based transmission analysis, a conditional
analysis showing that the IL23R signal comprises more than one
independent variant, a negative result at three related IL-23 pathway
genes, and cited mouse-model evidence supporting a therapeutic
rationale. All six items fit the schema as it stands; only one
candidate extension emerged, a direction-of-effect dimension
distinguishing protective from risk-conferring alleles. In line with
our two-papers rule (\cref{sec:workflow}), this extension remains
provisional until it is exercised by at least one additional
publication.

Two methodological points are worth highlighting in this example.
First, the \texttt{variant\_ascertainment} field records that
rs11209026 was observed in both cases and controls, which it was:
the case--control design ascertains variants in both groups by
construction. This use of the dimension is what justifies its
promotion to the core schema: the Duerr annotation exercises it, as
does the Davis annotation (patient-observed vs.\ functionally-tested
variants), and every variant-level annotation in the corpus requires
some ascertainment value. Second, each assertion carries a source
span pointing to a specific page and a short identifying phrase from
the paper, which is what makes the annotation auditable: a reader
disagreeing with our interpretation can check the underlying text
directly.

\subsection{A Polygenic Risk Score: Inouye et al.\ 2018}
\label{sec:ex-inouye}

The polygenic risk score literature stresses the model in a different
direction. Inouye and colleagues constructed a meta-genomic risk
score (metaGRS) for coronary artery disease from 1{,}745{,}180 SNPs,
weighted by effect size, combining three prior polygenic scores; they
validated the score externally in 482{,}629 UK Biobank participants,
reported a hazard ratio per standard deviation of 1.71, demonstrated
substantial independence from conventional risk factors, and showed
that the score's predictive power persists in medicated
subpopulations. Every one of these claims is a genuine piece of
genetic evidence, and every one of them stretches the model: the
target is not a gene or a variant but a derived score, the resolution
is not a locus but the whole genome in aggregate, and the measurement
targets are epidemiological (hazard ratio, C-index, cumulative
incidence) rather than molecular. The annotation surfaces six
candidate extensions as a direct consequence.

Four of the extensions (CE-IN1 through CE-IN4) propose, respectively,
a \dimval{Score} value for Target Type, a \dimval{Whole Genome
Aggregate} value for Resolution, a new \emph{target composition}
dimension distinguishing single versus aggregate targets, and an
extension of the measurement-target enumeration to cover
epidemiological quantities such as \dimval{Hazard Ratio} and
\dimval{C-Index}. Two further extensions (CE-IN5, on structured cohort
descriptors, and CE-IN6, on distinguishing natural from
derived-artifact targets) occur elsewhere in the annotation. The
methodological point is that where the model strains is itself
informative. Rather than silently forcing a polygenic score into a
variant-level field and producing a misleading annotation, the schema
requires the curator (human or AI) to mark the forced fit and propose
an extension. \Cref{sec:workflow} describes the promotion rule that
governs when such candidates become first-class, and
\cref{sec:discussion} reports what we learned from the pattern of
extensions surfaced across the six papers.
