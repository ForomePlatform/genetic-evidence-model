\section{Illustrative Examples from the Literature}
\label{sec:examples}

To demonstrate the model's coverage and to see where it strains, we
applied it to six published genetic-evidence papers that span the major
epistemic shapes encountered in literature-based variant interpretation
(Supplementary Table~ST9). The set covers classical Mendelian human
genetics (Gupta), population-scale association (Duerr) and case--control
resequencing (Davis), model-organism and \emph{in vitro} functional
genetics (Jossin, Nelson, and the functional arms of Davis), and a
genome-wide polygenic score (Inouye). Four of the six were annotated
manually from curator PDF highlights and callouts and are treated as
ground truth; the remaining two were drafted by an AI assistant and
curator-reviewed. All six annotations and per-paper case reports are
available in the project repository.\footnote{\url{https://github.com/ForomePlatform/genetic-evidence-model/tree/master/case-reports}}

Duerr et al.\ 2006 (\cref{sec:ex-duerr}) serves as our reference
example of a classical variant-level association study. Inouye et
al.\ 2018 (\cref{sec:ex-inouye}) is a polygenic risk score study
whose annotation deliberately strains the model and surfaces a
cluster of candidate extensions (\cref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{A Genome-Wide Association Study Example}
\label{sec:ex-duerr}

Duerr et al. 2006 identified IL23R as an inflammatory bowel disease gene through a genome-wide association study in
non-Jewish cases with ileal Crohn's disease, followed by independent replication and family-based transmission
testing. Under our decomposition, the paper yields seven \GeneticEvidence{} items (Supplementary Table~ST9 and Case
Report CR-DU): variant-level discovery and replication, a family-based transmission analysis, conditional
fine-mapping, negative results at related pathway genes, and cited functional and mouse-model
support. Six of the seven items fit the current schema without strain. The seventh, a cited anti-p40 antibody trial,
is drug-response evidence that exercises no existing genetic knowledge domain and therefore surfaces a
translational-scope gap. Review of this annotation also motivated three candidate extensions that remain
provisional under the two-papers rule: an explicit direction-of-effect dimension for protective vs risk-conferring
alleles, a construct for cross-item therapeutic synthesis, and an extension of the conditional-activation scope from
gene- to variant-level Mendelian context. Further detail is in Supplementary Note~SN6.1.


\subsection{A Polygenic Risk Score Stress Test}
\label{sec:ex-inouye}

Inouye et al. 2018 constructed a meta-genomic risk score (metaGRS) for coronary artery disease,
combining three component scores and testing performance in UK Biobank
participants. Unlike Duerr's variant-level association, the target here is a derived score spanning the whole genome,
and the main measurements are epidemiological. Our
annotation decomposes the paper into four \GeneticEvidence{} items: the score's predictive and stratifying power, its
independence from conventional risk factors, its persistence among medicated individuals, and the headline finding
that combining three component scores outperforms each
(Case Report CR-IN). All four are representable in the current model but only through deliberate ``least-wrong''
choices (for example, forcing the score into the variant target type and leaving variant ascertainment
not-applicable). These forced fits are recorded as six candidate
extensions, including a score target type, a whole-genome resolution, a target-composition axis
distinguishing single from aggregate targets, an epidemiological measurement-target vocabulary, a structured cohort
descriptor, and a flag for derived-artefact targets. Where and how the model strains is thus recorded openly rather
than papered over. Further detail is in Supplementary Note~SN6.2.
