\section{A Semantic Model of Genetic Evidence}
\label{sec:model}

The model comprises three parts: a small set of \emph{core classes}
(\cref{sec:model-classes}) that define what a unit of evidence is and
how evidence items compose; a \emph{dimensional vocabulary}
(\cref{sec:model-dimensions}) that describes each evidence item along
roughly twenty axes; and a \emph{conditional activation} mechanism
(\cref{sec:model-conditional}) that makes the vocabulary compact by
requiring dimensions only when they are semantically appropriate. The
three parts reinforce each other: the core classes are deliberately
minimal so the dimensional vocabulary can do the descriptive work, and
conditional activation keeps the vocabulary itself from becoming a
long list of dimensions that are ``required except when \ldots.''

\subsection{Core Classes}
\label{sec:model-classes}

Three abstract classes anchor the model:

\begin{description}
  \item[\ScientificEvidence] A unit of evidence, typically linked to a
    publication or a dataset, described by a set of dimensions (see
    \cref{tab:dimensions-core,tab:dimensions-cond}). A single paper may
    give rise to multiple
    \ScientificEvidence{} items when it reports evidentially
    distinct claims (for example, a case--control association and an
    in vivo functional assay for the same variant).
  \item[\EvidenceVariable] A named quantity extracted from one or more
    \ScientificEvidence{} items---for instance, the gnomAD allele
    frequency of a variant, the LOD score in a family, or the odds
    ratio reported by a GWAS. Variables are themselves annotated by
    dimensions, because the same quantity (say, an odds ratio) may
    carry different epistemic weight depending on how it was obtained.
  \item[\EvidenceAssertion] A predicate over one or more
    \EvidenceVariable{} instances, intended to be machine-checkable
    and composable in decision workflows. An assertion is a predicate
    of any polarity: it may state that a variant is associated with a
    phenotype, that it is \emph{not} associated, that its effect
    exceeds a threshold, and so on. The model places no syntactic
    restriction on the form of the predicate; it only requires that
    every assertion anchor to a specific source span in the underlying
    publication so that its provenance is auditable.
\end{description}

Specialising these three classes to the genetic domain yields
\GeneticEvidence, \GeneticEvidenceVariable, and
\GeneticEvidenceAssertion{} (\cref{fig:class-diagram}). The
specialisations inherit the provenance requirement and add the
dimensional annotations discussed next.

\subsection{Dimensions}
\label{sec:model-dimensions}

Each \GeneticEvidence{} instance is described along a vocabulary of
dimensions summarised in \cref{tab:dimensions-core}
(always-required dimensions) and \cref{tab:dimensions-cond}
(conditional dimensions). A handful of
dimensions are \emph{always required}---Knowledge Domain, Method,
Target Type, Resolution, and Credibility---because no well-formed
genetic-evidence item is fully specified without them. These are the
dimensions that tell a downstream reader \emph{what kind} of evidence
this is, \emph{how} it was obtained, \emph{what} it is about, and
\emph{how much} we should believe it.

Two further dimensions, Phenotype Scale and Variant Ascertainment,
were added to the core set during this work as a result of the
annotation process described in \cref{sec:workflow}. Phenotype Scale
distinguishes evidence about molecular phenotypes (e.g., binding,
activity) from evidence about cellular, histological, organismal, or
clinical phenotypes; the scale of the phenotype is independent of the
scale of the genetic target and is consequential for how the evidence
composes with other types. Variant Ascertainment records how the
variant under discussion entered the study---whether it was observed
in affected individuals, observed in controls, drawn from a database
of known polymorphisms, or constructed synthetically. Both dimensions
apply across the corpus of six annotated publications and are
discussed in \cref{sec:discussion}.

The remaining dimensions are \emph{conditional}. They are required or
meaningful only when other dimensions take particular values: it makes
no sense to ask for the mode of inheritance of a polygenic score, or
for the knockout type of a case--control human study. Rather than
marking these dimensions as ``optional'' and relying on convention,
the model attaches explicit activation conditions to each, formalised
as described in \cref{sec:model-conditional}. The activation condition
documents not just \emph{whether} the dimension is required, but
\emph{when}, which is a more useful piece of information for both
human curators and automated validators.

Credibility deserves brief separate comment. Unlike the other core
dimensions, Credibility is a fuzzy key--value field rather than a
closed enumeration: a curator may record any of an open-ended set of
credibility-relevant features (\texttt{cohort\_size},
\texttt{replication\_cohort}, \texttt{multiple\_testing\_correction},
and so on), accompanied by a free-text comment. The model deliberately
does not attempt to reduce credibility to a single numeric score, on
the grounds that clinical and research consumers of genetic evidence
have different weighting preferences over credibility features, and
collapsing them prematurely would lose information that could be
reconstructed downstream.

\subsection{Conditional Activation}
\label{sec:model-conditional}

The conditional activation mechanism is the least conventional part of
the model, and it is what makes the vocabulary compact without
sacrificing expressive power. Rather than declaring a fixed schema in
which every \GeneticEvidence{} item has every dimension (with many
dimensions marked as optional and left empty for most items), the
model declares that certain dimensions are required \emph{exactly
when} specified conditions hold over other dimensions. This mirrors
how domain experts actually reason: a human curator working through a
paper about an X-linked recessive pedigree knows to ask about mode of
inheritance, not because a global schema demands it, but because the
evidence being examined makes the question meaningful.

We formalise activation conditions as SHACL shapes using SPARQL-based
constraints, which are the idiomatic SHACL mechanism for expressing
requirements that cannot be captured by simple cardinality annotations
on a single property. Two examples, both drawn from the distributed
schema file, illustrate the pattern.

The first example encodes the rule that \emph{mode of inheritance} is
required when the Knowledge Domain contains \dimval{Human Genetics}
\textbf{and} the Target Type is \dimval{Gene}
(\cref{lst:shacl-moi}). Its logical shape is a conjunction over two
axes of classification.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=yaml, caption={SHACL conditional activation shape: \emph{mode of inheritance} is required when Knowledge Domain contains \dimval{Human Genetics} and Target Type is \dimval{Gene}.}, label={lst:shacl-moi}]
gem:ModeOfInheritanceConditionalShape
  a sh:NodeShape ;
  sh:targetClass gem:GeneticEvidence ;
  sh:sparql [
    sh:message
      "mode_of_inheritance is required when knowledge_domain contains HUMAN_GENETICS and target_type is GENE." ;
    sh:select """
      PREFIX gem: <https://w3id.org/genetic-evidence-model/>
      SELECT $this WHERE {
        $this gem:knowledgeDomain gem:HUMAN_GENETICS .
        $this gem:targetType      gem:GENE .
        FILTER NOT EXISTS { $this gem:modeOfInheritance ?moi }
      }
    """
  ] .
\end{lstlisting}

The second example encodes a different logical shape: \emph{organism}
is required when \textbf{either} the Method contains
\dimval{In Vivo Experiment} \textbf{or} the Knowledge Domain contains
\dimval{Model Organism} (\cref{lst:shacl-org}). Its activation is a
disjunction, and the two disjuncts involve different axes---one a
Method value, the other a Knowledge Domain value---so the rule also
illustrates that activation conditions can span the dimensional
vocabulary rather than being confined to a single axis.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=yaml, caption={SHACL conditional activation shape: \emph{organism} is required when Method contains \dimval{In Vivo Experiment} or Knowledge Domain contains \dimval{Model Organism}.}, label={lst:shacl-org}]
gem:OrganismConditionalShape
  a sh:NodeShape ;
  sh:targetClass gem:GeneticEvidence ;
  sh:sparql [
    sh:message
      "organism is required when method contains IN_VIVO_EXPERIMENT or knowledge_domain contains MODEL_ORGANISM." ;
    sh:select """
      PREFIX gem: <https://w3id.org/genetic-evidence-model/>
      SELECT $this WHERE {
        { $this gem:method gem:IN_VIVO_EXPERIMENT . }
        UNION
        { $this gem:knowledgeDomain gem:MODEL_ORGANISM . }
        FILTER NOT EXISTS { $this gem:organism ?org }
      }
    """
  ] .
\end{lstlisting}

Taken together, the two examples show that the activation conditions
encountered in practice are ordinary Boolean combinations over
dimension values: conjunctions, disjunctions, and
cross-axis constraints, all straightforwardly expressible in SHACL.
We have found that the small additional cost of writing each
conditional rule explicitly is repaid many times over in the
downstream clarity: a reader of an annotation file can look up
\emph{why} a particular dimension is required or absent, and a
validator can check conformance automatically. Critically, when an
annotated paper violates an activation condition (for example, a
human-genetics gene-level study that lacks a mode of inheritance
value), the validator reports not just that a field is missing, but
that a specific semantic condition has been declared and not met.

Section~\ref{sec:workflow} returns to conditional activation in the
context of the annotation workflow: we report there that every one of
the six publications we annotated could be placed into the model with
conditional activation acting as designed, and that the few cases in
which a curator found themselves populating a dimension the
conditional rules did not require---or leaving empty a dimension the
rules did require---were themselves informative signals about where
the model needed refinement.
